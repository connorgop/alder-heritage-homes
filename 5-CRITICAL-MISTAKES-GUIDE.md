## Mistake #1: Trusting a Wholesaler Instead of a Direct Buyer

### The Problem

When a wholesaler calls your house, they say: "We're a cash buyer. We can close in 7 days. We have proof of funds."

It sounds perfect. But it's a lie.

They're not a cash buyer. They're a middleman. They don't have cash. They have a rolodex of investors. They're searching for someone willing to pay more than their offer so they can take a cut.

### How the Scam Works

**Step 1:** They contact you and make an offer for $280,000.

**Step 2:** You sign a contract that says "and/or assignee" (buried in the fine print).

**Step 3:** They search for a real buyer willing to pay $300,000.

**Step 4:** They "assign" the contract to that buyer and take a $20,000 fee.

**Step 5:** You get $280,000. The real buyer pays $300,000. The wholesaler pockets $20,000.

**You lost $20,000 because you didn't know the difference between a wholesaler and a direct buyer.**

### How to Avoid This Mistake

**1. Check if they're a licensed real estate agent**
- Direct buyers are often licensed agents (regulated, accountable)
- Wholesalers rarely have licenses
- Ask for their license number and verify it with the California Department of Real Estate

**2. Ask for proof of funds in 24 hours**
- Direct buyers have cash and can prove it immediately
- Wholesalers will delay or make excuses
- Real proof of funds shows a bank statement with liquid capital

**3. Review the contract carefully**
- Look for "and/or assignee" language
- This phrase means they can assign the contract to someone else
- Direct buyers don't need this language (they're buying the home themselves)

**4. Ask if they're a direct buyer or wholesaler**
- Direct buyers will say "yes, we buy homes ourselves"
- Wholesalers will be vague or defensive
- Trust your gut

**5. Get a second opinion**
- Show the contract to a real estate attorney
- Ask a trusted real estate agent to review it
- Get an independent cash offer from another buyer

### Real Example: The Widow's Story

Maria inherited her husband's house. She was 73 years old and didn't want to be a landlord. She wanted cash and peace of mind.

A "cash buyer" company called her and made an offer for $250,000. She was excited. She signed the contract.

On day 6, they called her and said: "We need to reduce the price to $235,000 or the deal falls through."

She was devastated. She had already made plans for the $250,000. She needed that money.

She asked why the price was changing. They made up an excuse about inspection issues. It was a lie. They just couldn't find a buyer at $250,000.

She accepted the $235,000 because she had no choice. She was out $15,000 because she trusted a wholesaler.

**This is a common story. Don't let it be yours.**

## Mistake #2: Not Getting Proof of Funds Before Signing

### The Problem

You sign a contract with a "cash buyer." They promise to close in 7 days. You start making plans.

Then on day 5, they call and say: "We're still looking for financing. We need an extension."

Or worse: "We need to reduce the price or the deal falls through."

Why? Because they never had cash in the first place. They were searching for a buyer the whole time.

### Why Proof of Funds Matters

Proof of funds is a bank statement showing that a buyer has liquid capital available. It proves they can actually buy your home.

**Real cash buyers can provide proof of funds within 24 hours.** If they can't, they don't have cash.

### How to Verify Proof of Funds

**1. Ask for it in writing**
- Don't accept verbal promises
- Get it in the contract
- Make it a condition of the offer

**2. Verify it's real**
- Check the bank name and account number
- Look for recent dates (within 30 days)
- Look for the buyer's name on the statement
- Be suspicious of round numbers (e.g., exactly $500,000)

**3. Have a real estate attorney review it**
- They can spot fake documents
- They can verify it's sufficient for your home price
- They can ensure it's properly documented

**4. Don't accept promises**
- "We'll get proof of funds by Thursday" = They don't have it now
- "We're pre-approved for financing" = They don't have cash
- "We have investors lined up" = They don't have cash

### Red Flags

- They can't provide proof of funds within 24 hours
- The proof of funds is from a different person than the buyer
- The proof of funds is from a business account, not a personal account
- They ask you to sign before showing proof of funds
- They say "we'll provide it at closing"

### What Real Proof of Funds Looks Like

- Bank statement from a major bank
- Recent date (within 30 days)
- Buyer's name on the account
- Account balance exceeding your home price
- Bank letterhead with contact information

## Mistake #3: Accepting the First Offer Without Comparison

### The Problem

A cash buyer calls and makes an offer for $280,000. You're excited. You sign immediately.

Later, you find out another buyer would have paid $310,000.

You lost $30,000 because you didn't shop around.

### Why Comparing Offers Matters

Cash offers vary wildly. Some buyers are wholesalers taking huge cuts. Some are direct buyers with fair offers. Some are investors looking for deals.

**The only way to know if an offer is fair is to compare it to other offers.**

### How to Get Multiple Offers

**1. Contact 3-5 cash buyers**
- Get offers from direct buyers (licensed agents)
- Get offers from local investors
- Get offers from national cash buyer companies
- Spread them out over 1-2 weeks

**2. Ask the same questions to each buyer**
- "What's your offer price?"
- "What's your proof of funds?"
- "When can you close?"
- "Are there any contingencies?"
- "What are your fees?"

**3. Compare the offers side-by-side**
- Offer price
- Timeline to close
- Contingencies
- Fees
- Buyer credibility (license, reviews, references)

**4. Get a Broker Opinion of Value**
- Ask a licensed real estate agent for their opinion
- This shows you what your home is worth
- Compare cash offers to the market value
- If an offer is way too low, it's probably a wholesaler

### Real Example: The $30,000 Difference

Tom inherited a rental property. He got an offer from a "cash buyer" for $280,000. He was about to sign.

His daughter suggested he get a second opinion. He called another buyer.

The second buyer offered $310,000. Same property. Same timeline. $30,000 more.

Tom got a third offer for $305,000.

He accepted the highest offer ($310,000) and saved $30,000 by shopping around.

**Don't be the person who accepts the first offer.**

## Mistake #4: Ignoring Red Flags in the Contract

### The Problem

You sign a contract without reading it carefully. Later, you discover clauses that hurt you:

- "and/or assignee" (they can assign to someone else)
- Inspection contingencies (they can back out if they find issues)
- Appraisal contingencies (they can reduce the price if the appraisal is low)
- Financing contingencies (they can back out if they can't get financing)
- Price reduction clauses (they can ask for a lower price)

These clauses are red flags. They mean the buyer isn't confident in the deal.

### Critical Red Flags in Cash Contracts

**Red Flag #1: "and/or assignee"**
- This means they can assign the contract to someone else
- Direct buyers don't need this (they're buying the home themselves)
- Wholesalers use this to find end buyers

**Red Flag #2: Inspection contingency**
- This means they can back out if they find issues
- Real cash buyers buy homes as-is
- If they want an inspection contingency, they're not a real cash buyer

**Red Flag #3: Appraisal contingency**
- This means they can reduce the price if the appraisal is low
- Real cash buyers don't care about appraisals (they're paying cash)
- This is a sign they're looking for financing

**Red Flag #4: Financing contingency**
- This means they can back out if they can't get financing
- Real cash buyers don't need financing
- If they need financing, they're not a cash buyer

**Red Flag #5: Price reduction clause**
- This means they can ask for a lower price before closing
- Real cash buyers lock in the price
- This is how wholesalers trap sellers

**Red Flag #6: Long closing timeline**
- Real cash buyers close in 3-7 days
- If they need 30+ days, they're searching for a buyer
- The longer the timeline, the more likely they're a wholesaler

**Red Flag #7: Multiple contingencies**
- Real cash buyers have no contingencies
- Each contingency is a way for them to back out or reduce the price
- If there are more than 2 contingencies, be suspicious

### How to Review Your Contract

**1. Read every word**
- Don't skip sections
- Don't assume you understand it
- Look for hidden clauses

**2. Highlight red flags**
- Use a highlighter or pen
- Mark anything that seems unfair
- Mark anything you don't understand

**3. Ask questions**
- Ask the buyer to explain each clause
- Ask a real estate attorney to review it
- Ask another buyer if the clauses are normal

**4. Negotiate**
- Remove "and/or assignee" language
- Remove inspection contingencies
- Remove appraisal contingencies
- Remove financing contingencies
- Lock in the price (no reductions)

**5. Get it in writing**
- Don't accept verbal promises
- Get all agreements in writing
- Have a real estate attorney review it

### What a Real Cash Contract Looks Like

- No "and/or assignee" language
- No inspection contingencies
- No appraisal contingencies
- No financing contingencies
- No price reduction clauses
- 3-7 day closing timeline
- Proof of funds attached
- Buyer's license number (if applicable)

## Mistake #5: Not Understanding Your Net Proceeds

### The Problem

A cash buyer offers $280,000. You think you'll get $280,000.

But when you close, you get $250,000.

Where did the $30,000 go?

- Closing costs: $8,000
- Buyer's fees: $5,000
- Title transfer: $2,000
- Prorated taxes: $3,000
- Realtor commission: $12,000 (if you used an agent)

You didn't understand your net proceeds.

### Why Net Proceeds Matter

**Net proceeds** = The amount of money you actually receive after all fees, costs, and deductions.

This is different from the offer price.

### What Reduces Your Net Proceeds

**1. Closing costs (2-4% of sale price)**
- Title insurance
- Recording fees
- Escrow fees
- Transfer taxes
- Lien payoffs

**2. Real estate commission (if applicable)**
- Typically 5-6% of sale price
- Only if you used a real estate agent
- Not applicable if selling directly to a cash buyer

**3. Buyer's fees (if applicable)**
- Some buyers charge "processing fees"
- Some charge "inspection fees"
- Real direct buyers don't charge fees
- Wholesalers often charge hidden fees

**4. Mortgage payoff**
- If you have a mortgage, it gets paid off at closing
- This reduces your net proceeds

**5. Property taxes (prorated)**
- If you've paid taxes for the full year but only own the home for part of the year
- You get reimbursed for the portion you don't own

**6. HOA fees (if applicable)**
- If you have an HOA, fees get prorated
- You pay for the time you owned the home

**7. Liens or judgments**
- If you have tax liens, judgment liens, or other liens
- They get paid off at closing
- This reduces your net proceeds

### How to Calculate Your Net Proceeds

**Formula:**
Net Proceeds = Offer Price - Closing Costs - Buyer Fees - Mortgage Payoff - Prorated Taxes - HOA Fees - Liens

**Example:**
- Offer Price: $280,000
- Closing Costs: $8,000
- Buyer Fees: $0 (direct buyer, no fees)
- Mortgage Payoff: $150,000
- Prorated Taxes: $3,000
- HOA Fees: $0
- Liens: $0
- **Net Proceeds: $119,000**

### How to Maximize Your Net Proceeds

**1. Sell to a direct buyer (not a wholesaler)**
- Direct buyers don't take assignment fees
- You get more money

**2. Avoid buyers who charge fees**
- Some buyers charge "processing fees" or "inspection fees"
- Real direct buyers don't charge fees
- Avoid these buyers

**3. Negotiate the offer price**
- Get multiple offers
- Negotiate with the highest bidder
- Use the Price Match Guarantee (we'll match or beat any offer)

**4. Understand all costs upfront**
- Ask for a closing cost estimate
- Review it carefully
- Ask questions about any costs you don't understand

**5. Use a real estate attorney**
- They can review the closing statement
- They can identify hidden costs
- They can negotiate on your behalf
